# Criminal Justice Analysis: Wisconsin v. Ramirez (2016CF000031)

## 1. CHARGE PROGRESSION ANALYSIS

**Original Charges (chargeHistory):**
- Count 1: Battery by Prisoners, ¬ß940.20(1), Felony H
- Count 2: Disorderly Conduct, ¬ß947.01(1), Misdemeanor B

**Final Charges - NO CHARGES ADDED OR DROPPED:**
Both original charges proceeded to trial and conviction.

**Charge Modifiers Applied:**

*Count 1 - Battery by Prisoners:*
- **¬ß939.62(1)(b) - Repeater Enhancement** (increases maximum penalty)
- **¬ß939.63(1)(b) - Use of a Dangerous Weapon Enhancement** (adds 5 years to maximum)
- Both enhancers RETAINED at sentencing

*Count 2 - Disorderly Conduct:*
- **¬ß939.62(1)(a) - Repeater Enhancement** (misdemeanor level)
- Disposition notes: "**With use of a dangerous weapon enhancer dismissed**"
- The weapon enhancer was originally charged but dismissed at sentencing

**Significance:** The prosecution pursued aggressive enhancement charges. The dangerous weapon (a pencil, per trial exhibit) was deemed applicable to the felony but not the misdemeanor at sentencing.

## üéØ KEY FINDINGS & SIGNIFICANCE

### **Timeline Statistics:**
- **Offense to Filing:** 9 months
- **Filing to Trial:** 3 years, 10 months
- **Trial to Sentencing:** 2.5 months
- **Sentencing to Appeal:** 27 months
- **Appeal to Reversal:** 23 months
- **Reversal to Final Remittitur:** 16 months
- **TOTAL CASE DURATION:** **10 years, 5 months** (May 2015 - October 2025)

### **Critical Turning Point:**
The **September 26, 2018 demand for speedy trial** triggered statutory timelines. The subsequent 15-month delay to trial (December 2019) became the foundation for the **successful appellate reversal**, despite being denied twice at the circuit court level.

### **Ultimate Outcome:**
After a **guilty verdict at jury trial** and **15-year consecutive sentence**, the defendant's conviction was **REVERSED and DISMISSED** by the Court of Appeals on speedy trial grounds. The Wisconsin Supreme Court reviewed the decision but the remittitur was ultimately issued, **vacating the conviction after 5+ years of post-conviction litigation**.

This case exemplifies the constitutional significance of speedy trial rights, even for defendants already serving prison sentences.
